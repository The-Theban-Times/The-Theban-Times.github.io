The Ironic Life of The King of Thebes

By Saanvi Muthuchetty

The life of Oedipus was a very intricate, and ironic one. Much so that it could be told in the form of a story. Many of the major events in Oedipus’ life are examples of dramatic irony as the audience knows of Oedipus’ origins while Oedipus is still oblivious to his true identity.

       It was so nerve-wracking that Tiresias' foreshadowing came true and Oedipus purposely blinded himself and abandoned his kingdom. Oedipus knew only bits and pieces of his origins but yesterday it became crystal clear and it was something of his nightmares.

       The story that everybody knows was that Oedipus was the Prince of Corinth, son of Polybus and Merope. He had left his kingdom in hopes of not fulfilling a dreadful prophecy: he was to kill his father and sleep with his mother. On his journey of escape, he met a man that provoked him and then killed him. At this time Thebes was mourning the death of our King Laius and facing the wrath of the Sphinx. Finally, Oedipus arrived at Thebes and solved the great riddle which led the Sphinx to kill herself and free us. There he became our new king, married the widowed queen Jocasta and had four children.

       Yesterday, a messenger came to the palace bearing news of the death of Polybus Oedipus’s father. Oedipus was sad but also relieved that it was not him that killed his father as the prophecy had stated. Unfortunately, these thoughts were shaken when, in an effort to cheer the king up, the messenger told Oedipus that Polybus was not his true father. He had taken a baby from a shepherd and given it to the royal family as they had no sons.

       To solve the mystery he found the shepherd and what he said shook him. The shepherd confirmed the truth that Oedipus was the son of Laius. From there Oedipus connected the dots, and once again the words of Tiresias and the prophecy come true “'I say that you are the murderer you are seeking”, he was the murderer of King Laius and his very own father. From there on, Oedipus's past was revealed.

       Oedipus was the son of his wife Jocasta and the former King of Thebes Laius. Laius and Jocasta had heard about the prophecy of their son killing his father and ruining the bed of his mother. To try and stop this curse, they sent the baby with a shepherd. The messenger of Polybus found the baby and took him as the King had no sons. Oedipus was raised in Corinth with Polybus and Merope but he soon found out about the prophecy. In an effort to save his family, he left Corinth. On his escape, he ended up killing king Laius accidentally as he felt provoked by him. He arrived in Thebes, solved the riddle of the Sphinx, became King of Thebes, and married Jocasta. He killed his true father, slept with his true mother, and as hard as the prophecy tried to be stopped it found its way of coming true.
